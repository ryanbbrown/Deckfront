Ruleset candidate report

The best version is a contested map plus slower, answerable unit wins.

E013 is the strongest current direction: recruit cost 6, capped deck damage, a lead-4 response-window win condition, and a more contestable east/southeast map. It is not solved, but it produced the best strategic diversity and the most credible legal endings.

7 Validated full-game E013 runs, with multiple strategy families winning.
80 / 100 Top completed score shared by E006 and E013; E013 has stronger diversity evidence.
46+ Turn support games remain the main unresolved pacing failure.
Important caveat: old 16-turn checkpoint samples were not enough. Later full-game runs showed that some positions that looked stable at turn 16 converted into legal unit-count wins by turns 18-24.

Top Three Candidate Packages

1
E013 · 80

Contest-map lead-4 shell

rulesets/territory-v1-cost6-damagecap-responsewin-lead4 on maps/sketch-v3-contest.json

Best current direction Full-game evidence Strategy diversity Long support games P2 flank spike risk

This package keeps normal center income, raises recruit cost to 6, caps deck damage per attacker, and makes unit-count victories require a 4-unit lead that survives a full opponent response turn. The map moves east/southeast centers to create more contestable lanes.

What worked

  • P2 engine/healing beat P1 rush in three runs after 23, 27, and 31 turns.
  • P1 rush still had a real win path, winning one rush repeat after 22 turns.
  • P1 control stabilized against swapped P2 rush after P2 reached a five-center high-water mark.
  • Upgrade/support beat swarm/economy, proving slower support can convert.

What the games felt like

  • Threat windows were legible: pending leads were created, answered, cleared, and later confirmed.
  • Center splits mattered without making every game the same.
  • The downside is attrition: the support/swarm sample ran to 46 completed turns.
2
E006 · 80

Recruit cost 6 on access map

rulesets/territory-v1-cost6 on maps/sketch-v2-access.json

Cleanest simple fix Preserved board tension Checkpoint-era evidence Rush burst remained

E006 changed the least while moving the game in the right direction. It took the locked baseline and the improved access map, then raised recruit cost from 5 to 6 to slow supply-to-body conversion.

What worked

  • Center/flank stayed healthy: one run ended 7-6 on units, another tied 8-8.
  • Saved supply became meaningful instead of immediately turning into bodies.
  • One rush-vs-engine run kept P2 within one unit through turn 16.

What the games felt like

  • Cleaner and slower than the baseline without adding exception rules.
  • Still vulnerable to stacked deck-damage burst; one rush run reached a pending P1 win at 8-5 units.
  • Useful foundation, but less trustworthy than E013 because the runs stopped at the older 16-turn checkpoint.
3
E012 · 79

Lead-4 response-window shell

rulesets/territory-v1-cost6-damagecap-responsewin-lead4 on maps/sketch-v2-access.json

Best win-condition rule Cleaner than emergency defender P1 pressure persisted Superseded by E013 map

E012 is not the best final package, but it is the rules shell that made E013 possible. It kept the cost-6 economy and damage cap, then required a 4-unit lead to be pending and survive the opponent's response turn before winning.

What worked

  • Exact 3-unit leads stopped ending games.
  • Prior rush branches were delayed by 2-4 turns.
  • The rule created cleaner, more understandable counterplay than discounted emergency defenders.

What the games felt like

  • Better drama, but still mostly P1 conversion on the old access map.
  • When P1 held center control, games often walked from a denied 3-unit lead into a confirmed 4-unit lead.
  • The lesson was to keep the shell and fix map/economy pressure next.

Which Strategies Worked

Rush stopped being deterministic only after the E013 contest map.

Earlier variants slowed rush but did not reliably beat the center-income snowball. E007's damage cap made turn-16 positions look tied, but E007c full-game repeats showed P1 rush still won all three samples by turns 18, 20, and 24. E013 finally flipped multiple rush-vs-engine games: P2 engine/healing won three times while P1 rush still won once.

Flank play became real, then became slightly too sharp.

E005 and E006 improved center/flank tension by moving centers away from automatic P1 access. E013 went further and gave P2 flank/economy a real win path, including a 25-turn P2 victory with a 6-2 center split. That is good for diversity, but the 6-2 spike is also one of E013's main balance risks.

Support can win, but support-ball pacing is still the biggest flaw.

Upgrade/support was not deleted by the stronger economy and map rules. In E013 it beat swarm/economy, which matters. The cost was pacing: that game lasted 46 completed turns. E018's no-printed-healing stress test did not clearly solve this; one support run still lasted 50 turns and another stopped unresolved after 67 turns.

Control stabilization is possible, not guaranteed.

In E013's swapped rush/control run, P2 rush reached a dangerous five-center position, but P1 control stabilized and won after 28 completed turns. That is one of the strongest signals that E013 supports comeback play rather than only rewarding the fastest pressure line.

Near Misses And Lessons

E007

Damage cap looked excellent at the checkpoint, then failed full-game rush repeats. Three validated 16-turn bundles made rush pressure look contained, but E007c showed P1 rush still converted by turns 18, 20, and 24. Keep the damage cap as texture, not as the whole fix.

E015

Compressed income reduced spikes but weakened comeback elasticity. It softened some center conversion, but made support games worse at 58 turns twice and restored 20-turn P1 rush wins because defenders could kill but not also replace.

E016

Center-control wins shortened some games but were too permissive. A 23-turn support resolution was encouraging, but 11-turn and 19-turn center-control wins showed the threshold rewarded temporary high-water positions too much.

E017

Tight center-control removed the cheap wins and also removed the useful pressure. It still let rush confirm after 18-20 turns and support games stretched to 36 and 64 turns.

E018

No printed unit healing is a useful stress test, not yet a top candidate. The completed notifications show legal finishes in several runs: P1 rush wins at 18 and 30, P2 flank wins at 25 and 29, and one support win at 50. But one support run remained unresolved after 67 turns, so removing printed healing alone did not solve support pacing.
